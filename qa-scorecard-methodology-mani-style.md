# QA Scorecard Methodology — Grounded in Mani's Actual Call Style

This document explains, in full detail, how the Sales QA Scorecard is produced. It is calibrated against analysis of **53 of Mani's real growth consult calls** pulled from Fathom (Jan-Apr 2026, ~39,600 Mani utterances, ~5,700 questions he asked). Mani is the gold-standard closer reference — when Bronson and Krisz run calls, they are scored against how Mani would run that same call, using the patterns extracted below.

This is the third doc in the set:
1. Rebuild spec (standalone script architecture) — already delivered
2. Compliance + Prospect Rating methodology — already delivered
3. **This doc: QA Scorecard methodology** — how to score the 5 categories, what each score looks like, and how to generate the prose sections

---

## Overview: The Scorecard's 5 Categories

Every qualifying call gets a QA Scorecard with 5 categories, each scored 0, 1, or 2. Sum to a /10 total.

```
1. Frame Control              — /2
2. Discovery Depth            — /2
3. Authority & Positioning    — /2
4. Objection Handling         — /2
5. Close Attempt              — /2
```

Plus seven prose sections:
- Context paragraph (who, what, outcome)
- Closer vs Prospect Accountability
- What Went Well (2-3 bullets)
- Areas for Improvement (2-3 bullets with exact scripts)
- Single Biggest Mistake (with script)
- Missed Buying Signal
- Tone Warning
- Verdict

Never rename categories. Never use a different scale. The arithmetic must be shown.

For short follow-up calls (under 3 minutes), some categories may be N/A — normalize to /10.

## The Gold Standard: What Mani Does (Baseline Against Which Others Are Measured)

### Mani's actual patterns — extracted from 53 calls

**Frame Control signature phrases (appeared in 87% of his calls):**
- "Let me ask you a question."
- "Here's how I evaluate marketing."
- "Let me help you understand this."
- "Before we start spending any money, it first goes to you."
- "At the end of the four weeks, here's how I get paid."
- "Let me give you an example."
- "Let me see my data real quick."

**Discovery question set (his actual go-to questions, frequency from 53 calls):**

The big four numbers (he gets these on 60-77% of calls):
- "What was revenue in 2025, if you don't mind me asking?" (66%)
- "What's the goal in 2026 for revenue?" (75%)
- "What's your average ticket?" (58%)
- "What's your close rate?" / "How many do you close at?" (60%)

The bottleneck probe (his signature question, 47% of calls):
- "What do you feel like is the biggest bottleneck to scaling the business?"
- "What do you feel like is the biggest challenge with marketing?"

Structure probes:
- "How many markets do you operate in?"
- "What's company structure like?"
- "Is this in-house or an agency running it?"
- "Are you focused on insurance or retail? What percentage?"
- "What do you typically see as your gross profit margins?"
- "If you weren't to do any marketing this year, what would your revenue be at?"

Pre-call intel (he does this almost every call):
- He pulls up his data/client numbers before the call
- He references the AJ/assistant call or prior booking notes
- Example: "I was listening to your call with AJ. I guess what got you interested to reach out to us, man?"

**Authority & Positioning signature phrases:**
- "I do this all the time with my clients."
- "Let me take a look at my data real quick." (actually pulls up spreadsheet)
- "I pulled some data from some of the clients I have."
- "One of my clients in South Florida..."
- "Very similar to Klaus that we work with."
- "My CAC for a good client is $3,000."
- Name-drops specific clients: Good Guy Roofing (FL), Klaus network, etc.

**His actual pricing model (closer must match):**
- Base: **$4,000/mo + 10% of ad spend** (primary retainer)
- Second market/additional: **$2,000/mo + 10% of ad spend**
- Network/Klaus discount exists as a structured concession
- Setup fee exists per market (discounts for additional markets)
- Guarantee: "I'll give you a full refund if it doesn't work in two weeks."
- Ad spend to appointment benchmarks (the 20% margin rule):
  - $100/day ad spend → ~14 appts/mo
  - $150/day ad spend → ~21 appts/mo
  - $200/day ad spend → ~25 appts/mo

**ROI math he actually does on calls:**
- "$300 cost per appointment"
- "$17,800 in net margins off of $4,200 in ad spend"
- "At a 20% closing rate, my clients get $2,000 cost per acquisition"
- "The customer acquisition cost should be like $150 or $200"
- Does math LIVE, on screen share, with the prospect watching

**Pain / challenge reframes:**
- "The real issue isn't leads, it's that your audience size is too small."
- "At the end of the day, what we care about is CAC vs. profit margin."
- "It's like, can you get the donkey to become a stallion?" (metaphorical reframe)

**Case study usage (only 28% of calls actually used one):**
- Even Mani under-uses case studies. When he does:
  - "I'll tell you a story, [name], of one of my clients in South Florida."
  - "Let's take a look at one of my clients called Good Guy Roofing."
  - "I'll send you a folder of all my clients' best-performing ads."

**Close patterns:**
- Structured soft close: walks through onboarding timeline before asking for card
- "When everything's ready to go, I send it over to you."
- "Takes us about 5-7 business days after the onboarding call."
- "For onboarding tomorrow, be on a computer."
- Rarely uses false urgency. Uses calendar-based next step.
- Common closer phrasing: "Should we do tomorrow morning or afternoon?"

**When Mani discounts (structured, not cave-in):**
- Network member (Klaus) discount: pre-stated reason
- New market discount for existing clients scaling
- Setup fee reduction for multi-market deals
- NOT: panic-drop in response to "that's expensive"
- Price drops in 53% of calls, mostly structured. Almost never twice in one call.

**Tone signature (Mani's voice):**
- Peer-level, not deferential
- Uses "brother" / "dude" / "man" casually
- Mirrors prospect's intensity
- Doesn't chase or beg
- Direct challenges when warranted: "Why is it not being?" / "Why do you feel like you're on a downtrend?"
- Genuinely curious, not scripted

**Things Mani does that junior closers miss:**
1. He pulls up SPECIFIC market data for the prospect's city before the call
2. He asks "what would your revenue be if you did no marketing" — isolates organic baseline
3. He reframes price objections to value-per-dollar, not "can you afford it"
4. He locks in onboarding date before collecting card
5. He asks about company structure (employees, agency vs. in-house) to gauge scalability
6. He sets the ad spend expectation explicitly before quoting fee
7. He separates his fee from ad spend in pricing discussions

## Category 1: Frame Control (/2)

### What it measures

Does the closer run the call, or does the prospect run it? Frame control means the closer is leading the agenda, directing the flow, and setting expectations. It is NOT aggression — it is calm authority.

### Scoring rubric

**2/2 (Mani-level frame control):**
- Opens by setting expectations: "Here's how I like to run these calls..." or "Let me ask you a few questions first, then I'll walk you through what we do, and we'll see if it's a fit."
- Uses "let me" or "here's how" phrases to redirect when needed (Mani does this in 87% of calls)
- Stops prospect derails politely: "Good question, we'll get there in a second. First I want to understand..."
- Controls pacing: not rushed, not dragging
- Pivots to discovery even when prospect wants to jump to pricing
- Uses silence effectively after key questions
- If a prospect goes off-topic, pulls them back without friction

**1/2 (acceptable but passive):**
- Lets the prospect lead the structure but keeps the call moving
- Responds to questions rather than anticipating them
- Allows one major derail without reframe
- Doesn't set explicit agenda upfront but call still gets to the right places
- Pace is okay but sometimes reactive

**0/2 (frame lost):**
- Prospect runs the agenda end-to-end
- Closer answers pricing questions in the first 5 minutes before discovery
- Multiple topic derails with no reframe
- Closer sounds like they're being interviewed, not qualifying
- Prospect dictates next step instead of closer

### What to look for in transcripts

Search for these patterns in the closer's lines:

```
POSITIVE SIGNALS (count occurrences):
- "let me ask you" / "let me ask"
- "here's how I" / "here's how we"
- "before we get into" / "before we start"
- "first let me" / "the way I do this"
- "good question, we'll cover that in a sec"
- "I want to understand X first, then we'll talk about Y"

NEGATIVE SIGNALS (count occurrences):
- Closer answering pricing in first 10 minutes of transcript
- Multiple "well, what do you want to know?" from closer
- Prospect saying "can we just get to the point?" with no reframe
- Closer using "sorry" more than twice
- Closer asking prospect what the next step should be (instead of proposing one)
```

### Scoring examples from real calls

- **Scott Waldman call (Mani, 2026-04-07):** 2/2. Scott opened with complaints about the industry ("everybody says a lot of things, they don't deliver"). Mani validated briefly, then at 00:04:00 pivoted: "Understood. So that's been pretty much your experiences till now." Then at 00:02:14 locked agenda: "Obviously, Scott, I know you spoke to my assistant last week. I was listening to it. What got you interested to reach out to us, man?" Clean reframe.

- **Pinnacle Home Improvements call (Mani, 2026-04-08):** 2/2. Opened by asking about their relationship to Mark (the referral source), let them describe their understanding, then at 00:02:00 took over: "So to give you guys some background, RoofIgnite, what we do..." Bridged their context to his pitch without losing control.

- **Hypothetical 0/2 example:** closer greets prospect, prospect asks "so how much does this cost?" at minute 3, closer answers "well, it's about $4,000 a month," prospect grinds on price for 20 minutes, call ends with no discovery.

## Category 2: Discovery Depth (/2)

### What it measures

Did the closer get the information needed to qualify the prospect and build a relevant pitch? A surface-level "how's business?" doesn't count. Depth means specific numbers, pain points, and context.

### Scoring rubric

**2/2 (Mani-level discovery):**
Captures ALL of the "big five" plus 2+ "context deepeners":
- **Big five:**
  - Annual revenue (current year or last year)
  - Growth goal / target revenue for next 12 months
  - Current lead sources + what's working/not working
  - Average ticket size
  - Close rate or conversion on current leads
- **Context deepeners (need at least 2):**
  - Team size / structure (closers, installers, office)
  - Biggest bottleneck (the Mani signature question)
  - Current marketing spend and ROI
  - Gross profit margin
  - Insurance vs. retail split
  - Markets / service areas
  - Sales process (have one or winging it)
  - "What would revenue be with no marketing?" (organic baseline)

**1/2 (acceptable but shallow):**
- Gets 3 of the big five but misses ticket size or close rate
- Asks about pain but accepts first-surface answer without probing ("yeah, we need more leads")
- Doesn't verify numbers (prospect says "around $3M" and closer doesn't ask for the actual 2025 number)
- Misses the bottleneck question or asks it too late (after pitching)

**0/2 (discovery skipped or broken):**
- Less than 3 of the big five captured
- Closer starts pitching before asking about revenue, goals, or lead sources
- Accepts "we just need leads" as the complete problem statement
- Doesn't ask any follow-up to numbers given
- Treats the call as a product demo, not a qualification

### What to look for in transcripts

Count questions asked by the closer in the first 25% of the transcript. Mani averages 20+ discovery questions in his first quarter of the call.

```
MANI'S ACTUAL DISCOVERY QUESTIONS (pattern-match against transcript):
- "what was revenue in 2025"
- "how much revenue do you guys do"
- "what's the goal in 2026 for revenue"
- "what's your average ticket"
- "do you know the specific CAC"
- "what do you typically see as your gross profit margins"
- "where do you want the company to be"
- "what percentage of insurance vs retail"
- "how many markets do you operate in"
- "what's the company structure like"
- "what do you feel like is the biggest bottleneck"
- "what do you feel like is the biggest challenge with marketing"
- "if you weren't to do any marketing this year, what would your revenue be at"
- "is this agency running or in-house"

POSITIVE SIGNAL: closer asks 10+ questions in first quarter of call
NEGATIVE SIGNAL: closer pitches before asking 5+ of the above
```

### Scoring examples from real calls

- **Larry Brown call (Mani, 2026-03-03):** 2/2. Asked: "Guys, what's the goal in 2026 for revenue?" at 00:09:20. Probed Meta ads history at 00:07:01. Asked about canvassing at 00:08:31. Asked about any marketing-yielded revenue at 00:08:41. Full discovery before any pitch.

- **Jordan Hastings call (Mani, 2026-02-24):** 2/2. Asked: "Can I ask between us guys, why did you even decide to now want to grow a little bit and hire Melissa, get the help?" at 00:06:07. Asked about gross profit margins at 00:16:21. Structured discovery on why they're scaling now.

- **1/2 example:** closer asks revenue and goals but doesn't ask ticket size or close rate, then pitches on appointment volume. Missed conversion math because of missing data.

- **0/2 example:** closer opens with "so let me tell you what we do" in minute 2, never asks revenue.

## Category 3: Authority & Positioning (/2)

### What it measures

Does the closer establish credibility before being challenged on it? Authority is built through evidence, not claims. Positioning is the implicit message "you're lucky we might take you on" rather than "please pick us."

### Scoring rubric

**2/2 (Mani-level authority):**
- References specific clients by name and market (Mani: Good Guy Roofing South Florida, Klaus network, etc.)
- Shows actual data: "I pulled some data from my clients in Birmingham" and proceeds to reference specific numbers
- Uses "my clients" constructions naturally (Mani does this in 66% of calls)
- Takes posture of expert diagnosing, not salesperson persuading
- Willing to tell prospect NO ("honestly, I don't think we're the right fit for you right now, here's why")
- Implicit scarcity: mentions onboarding queue, capacity constraints, selection criteria
- Shares opinion forcefully when challenged: "No, that's actually wrong. Here's what's happening..."
- Gives away value unprompted (industry insights, specific tactics) to signal abundance

**1/2 (acceptable positioning):**
- Mentions "our clients" generically but no specific names or numbers
- Shares credentials but doesn't back with evidence
- Doesn't name-drop or case-study
- Holds own against light pushback but gets wobbly on hard pushback
- Claims expertise without demonstrating it

**0/2 (no authority):**
- Pitches features, not outcomes
- Defers to prospect's framing repeatedly
- Cannot answer detailed questions about results or data
- Sounds like a vendor asking for business
- Excessive qualifier language: "I think," "maybe," "hopefully," "we'll try"
- Apologizes when not warranted

### What to look for in transcripts

```
POSITIVE SIGNALS:
- "my clients" / "one of my clients" / "a client of mine"
- Named client references with geography or business detail
- Specific numbers ("my average CAC is $3,000")
- Industry observations delivered with certainty
- "Let me take a look at my data" followed by actual data share
- "I'll send you a folder of all my clients' best-performing ads"
- Disagreement with the prospect delivered cleanly
- Offering to disqualify: "honestly, if X is true, we're not a fit"

NEGATIVE SIGNALS:
- No client names or case studies in entire transcript
- Vague "we work with a lot of contractors" with no specifics
- Closer using "hopefully" / "I think we can" / "we'll try to"
- More than 2 apologies in the call
- Defers to prospect's definition of the problem without challenge
- Claims without backing ("we get great results")
```

### Scoring examples from real calls

- **Chad Brooks call (Mani, 2026-03-10):** 2/2. At 00:07:28: "I'll tell you a story, Chad, of one of my clients in South Florida." At 00:20:06: "Let's take a look at one of my clients called Good Guy Roofing." Walks through actual numbers with specific client context.

- **Philip York call (Mani):** 2/2. At 00:25:54: "I know my CAC for a good client is $3,000." Delivered as fact, not sales pitch. At 00:26:18 flipped to: "How much revenue do you want it to make in 2026?" — moved from authority to co-creating the plan.

- **Chuck Anania call (Mani):** 2/2 at 01:28:24: "I will send you a folder of all my clients' best performing ads so you can see what to actually replicate." Gives away value. Abundance-framed.

- **1/2 example:** closer says "we've worked with a lot of contractors," no names given, mentions results in general terms.

- **0/2 example:** closer cannot answer when prospect asks "can you name a client doing $3M+ in my state?" and pivots to pitch.

## Category 4: Objection Handling (/2)

### What it measures

When the prospect raises concerns (price, timing, trust, past bad experiences, non-DM status, "need to think about it"), does the closer handle them with reframe and evidence, or does the closer cave, panic, or argue?

### Scoring rubric

**2/2 (Mani-level objection handling):**
- Acknowledges the objection genuinely before responding: "Yeah, that makes sense, here's what I'd say..."
- Reframes from price to value-per-dollar or CAC vs. margin
- Uses specific data or client example to defuse
- For "need to think about it": identifies the real blocker with a trial close ("if the numbers made perfect sense today, would you want to move forward?")
- For non-DM: asks to pull DM onto the call
- For "we've tried agencies before": acknowledges the prior bad experience, separates self via specific differentiators (model: $4K + 10% ad spend, refund guarantee, data sharing)
- Does NOT drop price as first move
- Does NOT drop price twice in same call
- When a price drop IS used, it's structured ("for Klaus network members" / "multi-market") with reciprocal commitment

**1/2 (functional):**
- Acknowledges objection but response is generic
- Uses reframe but without specific evidence
- Drops price once but tied to a concrete concession (not a panic drop)
- Attempts trial close but doesn't isolate the real blocker
- Holds the line on obvious bad-fit objections

**0/2 (objection handling fail):**
- Drops price twice or more in one call without value reframe
- Arguments with prospect ("well actually...")
- Caves on key terms when pressure applied
- Avoids the objection, changes subject
- Offers discounts or free add-ons unprompted
- Says "I totally understand" and moves on without addressing

### What to look for in transcripts

Identify every prospect objection, then examine the closer's immediate response in the next 1-3 lines.

Objection categories and expected responses:

```
OBJECTION: "That's expensive" / "Our budget is tight"
CORRECT: Reframe to CAC vs. margin. "Let's back up. You said your average ticket
         is $12K and your margin is 35%. That's $4,200 gross per job. At 20% close
         rate on my appointments, one deal covers my fee plus your ad spend. Expensive
         compared to what?"
WRONG:   "Okay, I can do $3,500 instead of $4,000."

OBJECTION: "I need to think about it" / "Let me discuss with my partner"
CORRECT: Trial close. "If the numbers made perfect sense and we had a guarantee
         in place, would you want to move forward? Good. Then let's get your
         partner on the phone right now or tomorrow morning."
WRONG:   "Of course, take your time, let me know when you're ready."

OBJECTION: "We've tried agencies before, they all suck"
CORRECT: Acknowledge, separate. "Yeah, I've heard that a lot. What did they charge?
         Flat fee? Right. That's why they don't care about performance. My model is
         $4K + 10% of ad spend, so if you don't spend, I don't get paid. I'm
         incentive-aligned. Also: 2-week money-back."
WRONG:   "Well, we're different, we actually care about our clients."

OBJECTION: "Let me check with the CEO"
CORRECT: "Text him right now. Ten minutes on this call. If he approves, we lock
         today's pricing. If not, no worries, we book a call."
WRONG:   "Yeah, get back to me when you can."

OBJECTION: "How do I know this will work?"
CORRECT: Specific client example with numbers + guarantee. "Good Guy Roofing in
         South Florida, started with us at $X/mo ad spend, hit $Y cost per appointment
         in week 3. Here's the actual dashboard. Plus the 2-week refund if it doesn't hit."
WRONG:   "We have a great track record."
```

POSITIVE SIGNALS:
- "Let me reframe this for you"
- "The real question is..."
- "Here's an example of a client who had the same concern"
- Trial close language ("if X, would you Y?")
- Structured discount with reciprocal commitment
- Inviting DM onto the call

NEGATIVE SIGNALS:
- Multiple price drops in same call (count occurrences of "I can do" / "I'll knock off" / "take off")
- "I totally understand, let me know when you're ready"
- Apologetic responses to pushback
- Adding free services unprompted
- Arguing ("well actually")

### Scoring examples from real calls

- **Scott Waldman call (Mani, 2026-04-07):** 2/2. Scott opened with heavy objection ("everybody says a lot of things, they don't deliver... the only thing they ever deliver is my credit card"). Mani responded at 00:03:59 with "Understood. So that's been pretty much your experiences till now." Then used his performance-based model ($4K + 10% of ad spend) as the differentiator. Flipped a scar into a positioning advantage.

- **William Ipsan call (Krisz, earlier this week):** 1/2. Dropped price twice in one call ($2,500 off, then another $500) when the real blocker was CEO sign-off, not affordability. Correct response: pull CEO onto the call. This is the canonical "price is the wrong lever" mistake.

- **0/2 example:** prospect says "that's expensive." Closer responds: "okay I can do it for $3,000." Prospect: "still a lot." Closer: "how about $2,500 plus I'll throw in the onboarding free?" No reframe, pure cave.

## Category 5: Close Attempt (/2)

### What it measures

Did the closer actively ask for the commitment, propose a concrete next step, and attempt to move the deal forward? A call that ends with "let me know what you think" is not a close.

### Scoring rubric

**2/2 (Mani-level close):**
- Direct ask: "Ready to move forward?" / "Should we get you set up?" / "Want to lock this in?"
- If yes: pivots to logistics — card collection, onboarding call booking, timeline
- After card collected: walks through onboarding timeline, introduces delivery team, sets first-30-days expectations (this is the post-close sell — critical)
- If not yet: proposes specific next step on calendar — "Tomorrow 2pm with your CEO" not "let me know"
- Handles "I'll think about it" with trial close + specific time commitment
- Uses assumptive language: "When we launch, you'll want to..." not "if we end up working together"

**1/2 (functional close):**
- Asks for commitment but not directly
- Proposes next step but isn't specific on time
- Collects card OR books next step (not both when both are possible)
- Post-close expectation-setting is brief or skipped

**0/2 (no close):**
- Call ends with "let me know what you think" or equivalent
- No next step booked
- No card collected, no calendar booking, no commitment isolated
- Closer leaves the meeting in the prospect's hands entirely
- No trial close attempted

### What to look for in transcripts

Look in the last 20% of the transcript.

```
POSITIVE SIGNALS:
- Direct close questions in the last quarter
- "Should we get you set up"
- "Ready to move forward"
- "Let's book onboarding for tomorrow"
- "What's better, morning or afternoon?" (assumptive choice)
- "I'll send you the invoice right now"
- Specific calendar time proposed
- Post-close: "When everything's ready to go" / "Takes 5-7 business days"
- Post-close: "For onboarding tomorrow, be on a computer"
- Post-close: introduction to delivery team or account manager

NEGATIVE SIGNALS:
- Call ends without any direct ask
- "Let me know what you think"
- "Take your time"
- "Reach out when you're ready"
- Vague "we'll talk soon" with no booked time
- No card collected AND no calendar appointment booked
```

### Scoring examples from real calls

- **Kevin Cesare call (Mani):** 2/2. Closed at 00:21:34 with: "Okay, so tomorrow morning or tomorrow afternoon, you're meeting with Cole." Assumptive, specific, calendar-based. Then at 00:21:25: "Just make sure tomorrow you show up on a computer and that's pretty much it." Post-close expectation set.

- **Pinnacle Home Improvements call (Mani):** 2/2. Locked in at 01:27:45 with "Should we email or text?" (assumptive close on delivery method for the contract).

- **William Ipsan call (Krisz):** 1/2. Got Will to commit to "text back by 7:30 pm" but no card collected. Partial close. Missed the real close by not pulling CEO onto the call during the 10-minute window.

- **0/2 example:** closer ends call with "thanks for your time, let me know if you have any questions." No next step. No card. No booked time.

## Scoring Adjustments and Special Cases

### Short follow-up calls (under 3 minutes)

Some categories become N/A. Score what's applicable, exclude the rest from the arithmetic, normalize to /10.

Example: 2-min decline call. Frame Control and Objection Handling are scoreable. Discovery Depth, Authority & Positioning, and Close Attempt are N/A (no discovery needed, no pitch needed, no full close applicable).

```
Scoreable: Frame 1/2 + Objection 1/2 = 2/4 → normalize: (2/4) × 10 = 5/10
```

### Follow-up calls with the same prospect

Score in the context of the full engagement, not just this call. If the original call was handled well and this is a decline follow-up, the closer gets credit for the work they did across both. Note in Verdict: "Across both calls, closer moved prospect from X to Y."

### Training / coaching calls

These are NOT QA'd. Skip with: "Internal team meeting detected. Only prospect-facing calls are QA'd."

### Calls with multiple prospects

If 2+ external attendees, assess ability to manage group dynamics. Note imbalance: "Samantha engaged, Tommy quiet — closer should have pulled Tommy in at X."

## Prose Section Generation

### Context paragraph (1-2 sentences)

Template: "~[duration]-min [call type] with [prospect] from [company/market]. [Closer] [summary of what happened]. Outcome: [card collected / follow-up booked / no commitment / declined]."

Example: "~38-min same-day re-engagement. Krisz executed the priority action from Call #1 feedback (recruiting upsell) and got William back on Zoom with a bundled $4,500 marketing + hire offer, later dropped to $4,000. Outcome: Will needs to clear the funds with CEO, committed to text back by 7:30 pm. No card collected."

### Closer vs Prospect Accountability (MANDATORY SECTION)

This is the guardrail that prevents blaming closer for prospect-driven outcomes.

```
CLOSER-OWNED ISSUES: [2-3 sentences. What the closer actually did wrong or could
improve, tied to specific transcript moments with timestamps. These are skill
gaps, not bad luck.]

PROSPECT-DRIVEN FACTORS: [1-2 sentences. Things outside closer's control: non-DM
status, budget gone for the year, wrong vertical, already signed elsewhere.
These DO NOT count against the closer in scoring.]
```

### What Went Well (2-3 bullets)

Each bullet: ONE sentence identifying the strength + timestamp reference. Explain WHY it was effective.

DON'T paraphrase entire exchanges. DON'T reproduce multi-line transcripts. ONE sentence per bullet.

Examples:
- "Same-day re-engagement at [17-20] 00:02:19 executed exactly what Call #1 QA asked for — recruiting pilot converted into a bundled offer with a compressed May 3-7 launch."
- "Trial close at [303] 00:20:39: 'If the funds weren't an issue, would you want to do this today?' — textbook commitment test that isolated the real objection."

### Areas for Improvement (2-3 bullets)

Each bullet: ONE sentence identifying the gap with transcript reference, PLUS ONE sentence of exact script of what the closer SHOULD HAVE SAID (in quotes).

Keep scripts to 1-2 sentences, not full paragraphs.

Examples:
- "Real math on paper before the call — gap at [78-84]. Script: 'I'll pull up the numbers so we both see them: $3K marketing + $2.5K hire = $5.5K, minus $1.5K discount = $4K flat for everything.'"
- "Pull the CEO onto the call instead of dropping price — gap at [295-302]. Script: 'Text him right now — 10 minutes on this call, he sees the numbers, we move forward today at the original price.'"

### Single Biggest Mistake (2-3 sentences max)

Format: what happened (with transcript reference) + what the closer should have said instead (1-2 sentence script in quotes).

If no critical error, state that.

Example: "At [317-318] 00:21:34 Krisz dropped another $500 ('I'll give you another $500 off') right after Will cited cashflow. Price-drop is the weakest lever when the block is sign-off, not affordability. Script: '$4,500 stands — but if your CEO approves on this call, I'll throw in the first-week ramp coaching for your rep at no cost.'"

### Missed Buying Signal (1-2 sentences)

Format: identify the exact moment prospect showed interest, what was said, what the closer should have done.

If no buying signals, state: "None — prospect showed no buying signals."

Example: "At [273] 00:22:43 Will said 'I like the aggression, Krisz' — that's a yes-frame. Should have pivoted to a hard ask: 'Good — get your CEO on the phone in the next 10 minutes and we lock it.'"

### Tone Warning (1 sentence)

Flag neediness, desperation, over-aggression.

Common patterns to flag:
- "I'm just trying to help" repeated
- "Please" used more than twice
- Voice rising into questions at the end of statements
- Apologizing more than twice
- Talking over the prospect
- Asking for the sale more than 3 times in 5 minutes

If tone is clean: "None — tone was steady and peer-level."

### Verdict (2-3 sentences)

1. Overall assessment
2. What happens next (concrete action)
3. Most important coaching takeaway

Example: "Across both calls today, Krisz moved William from 'I will reach back out' (soft off-ramp) to 'I'll text you by 7:30 with a decision' (soft commit) — real progress. Priority next step: if no text by 8 pm, Krisz calls Will directly and offers to jump on a 3-way with the CEO tonight. Do not drop price again."

## Quick Reference: The 5-Category Rubric Card

```
FRAME CONTROL (/2)
  2 = opens with agenda, redirects derails with "let me ask" phrases,
      paces call, stops pricing questions until discovery done
  1 = call stays on track but closer is reactive
  0 = prospect runs the agenda; closer answers pricing in first 10 min

DISCOVERY DEPTH (/2)
  2 = all 5 big numbers + 2+ context deepeners + bottleneck probe
      (revenue current, revenue goal, lead sources, ticket size, close rate,
       plus team/margin/markets/etc.)
  1 = 3 of 5 big numbers + surface pain
  0 = less than 3 big numbers; pitch before discovery

AUTHORITY & POSITIONING (/2)
  2 = named client references with data ("my client in FL does X CAC"),
      willingness to disqualify, expert diagnosis tone, abundance framing
  1 = generic "my clients" without specifics, claims without backing
  0 = no social proof, excessive qualifier language, vendor tone

OBJECTION HANDLING (/2)
  2 = acknowledge + reframe + evidence; trial close on "think about it";
      structured discount only (never panic-drop); max 1 price drop ever
  1 = generic acknowledgment + reframe without data
  0 = 2+ price drops; arguing; caving; avoiding the objection

CLOSE ATTEMPT (/2)
  2 = direct ask + assumptive logistics + card OR booked call + post-close
      expectations set (onboarding timeline, delivery team intro)
  1 = asks but vague; books OR collects card (not both when possible)
  0 = "let me know what you think" — no next step, no card, no calendar time
```

### Hard enforcement in Python (not the LLM)

The LLM returns category scores (0/1/2 or null). Python does:

1. Sum the non-null category scores
2. Divide by 2 × number of non-null categories
3. Multiply by 10
4. Round to integer
5. Verify: sum arithmetic in the rendered message matches the scores

If the rendered message arithmetic does NOT match the scores, reject and re-prompt. This catches LLM hallucination on the math.

## Data Provenance

Patterns, frequencies, and example references above are extracted from 53 growth consult calls in Mani's Fathom account, covering 2026-02-13 to 2026-04-14. Total analysis footprint: 39,678 Mani utterances across 5,744 questions. Pattern-frequency data:

```
Frame control phrases          — 87% of calls

Goal/vision question           — 75% of calls
Ad spend discussion            — 74% of calls
Revenue question               — 66% of calls

Close rate question            — 60% of calls
Ticket size question           — 58% of calls
Structured price concession    — 53% of calls
Bottleneck / pain probe        — 47% of calls
Case study reference           — 28% of calls (under-used even by Mani)
```

Two insights worth flagging for coaching:

1. **Even Mani under-uses case studies** (28%). If the team increases case-study usage to 50%+, close rates should improve. This is a systemic coaching opportunity, not just an individual scoring dimension.

2. **Mani rarely uses high-pressure urgency** (commitment language in only 25% of calls). His close style is calendar-based and assumptive, not scarcity-driven. Closers who try to create false urgency are violating Mani's style and should be flagged in Tone Warning.
